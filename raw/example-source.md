# The Feynman Technique

**Source type:** Article / Learning methodology  
**Origin:** Derived from the practices of physicist Richard Feynman  
**Relevance:** Learning, knowledge management, teaching

---

The Feynman Technique is a four-step method for learning any subject deeply, named after Nobel Prize-winning physicist Richard Feynman, who was known as much for his ability to explain complex ideas simply as for his scientific achievements.

## The Four Steps

**1. Choose a concept and write it down.**  
Pick something you want to understand. Write the concept name at the top of a blank page.

**2. Explain it as if teaching a child.**  
Write out an explanation of the concept using plain language, as if you were teaching it to someone with no background in the subject. Avoid jargon. Use analogies and concrete examples.

**3. Identify gaps and return to the source.**  
Where your explanation breaks down or becomes vague, you've found a gap in your understanding. Go back to the source material and study those gaps specifically. Repeat until you can explain cleanly.

**4. Simplify and use analogies.**  
Refine your explanation further. If you're still using technical terms you couldn't define in plain language, replace them. Find an analogy from everyday life.

## Why It Works

The technique forces active recall rather than passive re-reading. Writing out an explanation exposes the difference between recognition (feeling like you know something) and recall (actually being able to produce it). The "child" framing eliminates the hiding place of technical vocabulary — you cannot fake understanding when you must explain from first principles.

Feynman himself described this as the difference between knowing the name of something and knowing something. Knowing that a bird is called a "brown-throated thrush" in one language and another name in another language tells you nothing about the bird itself — its behavior, its biology, its place in the world.

## Connection to Knowledge Systems

The Feynman Technique maps naturally onto note-taking systems like Zettelkasten: both emphasize writing in your own words, linking ideas, and surfacing gaps. A wiki-style knowledge base benefits from this discipline — every page should be explainable in plain language, and gaps in the wiki reveal gaps in understanding of the source material.

## Key Figures

- **Richard Feynman** — physicist, Nobel laureate (1965), Caltech professor; known for the Feynman Lectures on Physics and his unconventional teaching style
- **Cal Newport** — popularized the technique in *How to Become a Straight-A Student* and related writing

## Limitations

The technique is most useful for conceptual understanding. It is less directly applicable to procedural skills (e.g., playing an instrument) where understanding and performance are separate. It also requires you to already have access to source material to fill gaps — it is a learning method, not a research method.
